Project Summary

A semester long class focused on designing and creating a game based off student choice. The class was structured in a way to provide students with as much control as possible, leaving the professors as simply the guard rails and advisors for the producer. The goal was to create a playable demo to then be presented at the end of the semester for everyone to play and give feedback on.

Core Responsibilities

A set of baseline rules and structure were organized as follows:

  • Created multiple sources of communication such as Discord, Trello and Miro for each team to use. Discord was the main point of contact for everyone.
  • Team leads were to write up a progress report every 2 weeks to document pacing and overall problems/concerns that need to be addressed with me or other team members.
  • Throughout off time during production, I helped to set up the core structure for the codebase alongside the programmers. Some example systems were the core battle state engine and enemy/power selection (UI to system integration).
  • Communicated often with professors to keep the timeline of production in check. This ensured the scheduling was on pace for an alpha version of the game to be completed within the semester.
Core Issues, Solutions, and Things I'd Do Differently

Most members were brand new to the process of video game production, however many artists have worked on team projects prior. Leveraging that prior knowledge was crucial to starting a smooth workflow between all members.

  • Discord, Miro, and Trello were used in tandem for note keeping and communication outside of class hours. Each tool was set up to be organized to easily identify everybodies role and helpful channels to keep topics within their own dedicated spaces.
    • With many tools at the ready for use, some members were unsure of where to post their progress and or ideas. In the future, I’d like to clarify which tool should be used for what, and leave more specific tools such as Miro (mainly the artists used) up to be created by the team leads themselves. That way I still have an overview of all overhead info, while giving control to my leads.

Many artists inquired on guidance for art direction which created many situations where work was being constantly double and triple checked by one person, causing a lot of strain on the narrative lead who created the original concept of the project.

  • A meeting was held in place to better direct some of the frustrations and responsibilities of the narrative lead to the art lead when it came to styling and final checks.
    • Funneling info and questions to the lead before getting input from other roles should have been established first. That way leads can organize everything that needs to be solved and be taken up to me or the lead depending on the circumstances.

Scope creep was a very real issue. While this was kept in mind throughout the entire semester, there was still a level where of unknown due to the fact that people had varying skill and speed levels.

  • This made learning everyones work ethic and pacing an initial challenge. In all honesty I should have set very concrete goals biweekly, and communicated with the narrative lead on more of the core must haves to make sure would be done.
    • In the future, I’d like to set up a more concrete schedule with biweekly goals and checkpoints to make sure we are on pace for the alpha version of the game. This would also help with learning the pacing of each team member and adjusting accordingly. It would allow for the project to be guided to something that is more feasible to accomplish.

Having one sound engineer put a lot of load on their shoulders, it very quickly became a point of bottleneck.

  • With this in mind I ensured that the tasks they were responsible for were purely for the core alpha. We sat down and discussed what was the most important, feasbile, and what to push against scope wise.
    • In the future identifying the bottleneck in any area of the project as early as possible will give me a good indicator as to how to pace the project. With smaller teams it's incredibly important to ensure that the essentials are being worked on at any given moment. As mentioned with scope creep the project can quickly spiral into teams working on different sections of a project at the same time causing disjointed progression.
Internship - Puzzle Game Foundation

An internship program directed by Dr. Jason Shepherd that tested my abilities to create material for future game development courses and peers. This program tested my ability to quickly create a small project along with a write up that balances both technical implementation and non technical subjects such as design and game structure to students from multiple disciplines. The project I decided to create would be a simple 3D puzzle game with minimal mechanics to provide the easiest vessel for information.

Initial discussions were held to determine the overall direction of the project. I was given an opportunity to brainstorm and pitch multiple ideas and themes that would pair nicely with prior course work that didn't have many overlapping points of subject. I decided on creating a basic 3D puzzle scene that primarily focused on cause and effect mechanics (such as press a button, something happens).

Students would be given the package that I shipped out without most of the scripts that made the project work, this is where the write up came into play to help students rig up the project together and get a basic understanding of how to create the pieces for their own section.
